As always, please understand that his interpretation of the book is purely my own and it is definitely biased and not the _answer._ This also contains a bit of spoilers, so don't read this post if you do not want spoilers.

I also didn't fully understand the book while I was reading it, so if there is any correction I would be happy to take it.

Recently, I have been living as if I was full of confidence, and I've been trying to force myself into thinking that I was an amazing person, and I think I successfully tricked myself. It was an act of me trying to counteract how I was just failing everything and my confidence was hitting the floor, and I honestly didn't think I was doing this to the point where I was absolutely shocked after reading The Fall because I really believed that I was an amazing person. 

There was a couple of reasons why I thought I was an amazing person, and it's mostly because I felt superior to others because I easily done tasks that my friends in high school cannot do, and I thought I looked cool like attempting things and just thinking deeply into things. If someone suggested that I was wrong, then I would always get offended first rather than correcting what I was doing, and if someone complemented me, then I wouldn't really be any happy or anything, and I would think it was obvious. I mean this isn't really absolutely what happened, but now that I think about it, I think it is plausible that I was full of myself just to protect myself.

So I finished the book, unsure what the book was trying to talk about, and I watched a explanation video, and it was just so surprising that I started having a headache. I might have been the perfect target for Clamence's lessons, because his first part, I could totally relate to although the process was a bit different. It was good because it helped me realize that I thought I was someone amazing when I was not and I was actually full of flaws.

The funnier thing is, I am doing everything I want to do, contrary to how Clamence just started doing things he enjoyed doing after realizing that he was living a fake life. Like It was just me doing whatever I want to do, things like playing video games for 3 hrs and just watching tv the next 2 hrs, and still thinking, that I am an amazing person. It is fake, and it should not lead me to thinking that I am an amazing person. But it's not really leading me into doing things I dislike really. 

But now I do think that I should step away from thinking that I am an perfect individual and everything I do should be justified. It's just adding ego to myself, while doing nothing to keep my self esteem high and it just creates unnecessary situations when interacting with others. It makes me very lazy, and the stance that I'm a perfectly fine individual no matter what, is just not a good stance in the point of view of improving and living my life.

I realize after reading the Stranger that it might be the truth for me that I do not need to improve myself and I am just a fine person. But if it is the truth, I just want to go ahead and start believing it later in my life, after I have lived my life and fully discovered it. I wouldn't want to just suddenly be stuck on one belief and just give up on life and become an harm for my relatives. Maybe something that I can ponder and believe in when I am like 60 years old, because I probably wouldn't even regret it if I discovered my true belief that late, because I would understand that I needed that time to discover it.

So what am I going to do about persuading myself into thinking that I am a perfect individual? I am going to stop doing that. Lately I have been pondering around the concept me faking myself into believing certain things, and I think the best way to discover why I did those things is to stop doing it.
